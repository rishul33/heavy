September 30, 2022

The Nobel Goes to…

Announcements for this year’s Nobel Prize winners will begin on Monday. In the meantime, learn about one of the most prestigious awards in the world.
“Merchant of Death”
In 1888 a French newspaper, mistakenly believing Alfred Nobel had died, wrote an obituary that used this unfortunate nickname. Discover the story behind the moniker and how it likely gave rise to the awards.
Think you deserve a Nobel?
Then this article is for you! Learn all about the selection process and why it helps to befriend a former winner.
Pomp and controversy?
While the awards might seem staid, they have not been free from scandal. Read about a few controversies, including the time Hitler was nominated for the Peace Prize.
Has anyone turned down the award?
Surprisingly, two people have, and this French writer was the first. The other was Le Duc Tho, who turned down the 1973 Peace Prize (though his corecipient accepted).
Who was the first woman to win?
Find out that answer and more in our quiz.

Brazil on the Brink

On October 2, with the threat of violence looming, a deeply polarized Brazil will choose between two starkly different presidential candidates, authoritarian incumbent Jair Bolsonaro and leftist icon and former president Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva. Without evidence, Bolsonaro has sought to cast doubt on the country’s electronic voting system. If neither contender captures more than 50 percent of the vote, there will be a runoff on October 30.

Ian on the Move

With a sustained wind speed of 155 miles per hour and a record storm surge, this “catastrophic” category 4 hurricane is among the most powerful storms to make landfall in the United States. It has already left all of Cuba without power, and more than 2.5 million Floridians are under an evacuation order. It will be days before we know the full extent of the damage.

The Rich Didn’t Actually Get Richer This Year

Forbes magazine is out with its annual list of the richest people in America, and for the first time since the Great Recession, the richest people actually got poorer. As a group, the so-called Forbes 400 is $500 billion poorer than they were in 2021. Before you feel sorry for them, however, consider this: their combined net worth is $4 trillion. That’s a 4 with 12 zeros. The top 3 on the list are familiar names, but do you know how they garnered their wealth?

Far Right Ascendant in Europe?

The apparent victory of Giorgia Meloni’s Brothers of Italy party in Italy’s general election has raised questions about the growing influence of the far right in Europe.
Heirs to Mussolini's legacy
Although Meloni claimed "fascism is history," her party was founded by postwar supporters of Mussolini. And like Mussolini's Fascist Party, the Brothers of Italy Party embraces xenophobic rhetoric and the subordination of individualism to nationalism.
“Ethnic substitution”
Meloni has promoted this racist conspiracy theory as part of her anti-immigration platform.
A political earthquake in Sweden
Swedish Prime Minister Magdalena Andersson resigned earlier this month after a party with neo-Nazi roots became the second-biggest bloc in parliament.
Villifying Brussels
A common thread that binds many within Europe’s far right is a strain of populist nationalism that rejects the rules and structures of the EU.

A Spring Without Birdsong?

Today is the 60th anniversary of the publication of Silent Spring by Rachel Carson, one of the seminal works of environmental writing. The book highlights the dangers of chemical pesticides, especially DDT, on insect, bird, and human populations, while also calling out the chemical industry’s disinformation. As we continue to grapple with pollution in all of its forms—from plastic waste to fracking to greenhouse gases—the work remains relevant in its skepticism of scientific progress and of the conceit that humans should exert control over nature.

Iran’s Iron Grip

Demonstrations broke out across Iran last week after 22-year-old Mahsa Amini died in custody for “improper” dress. The incident was the latest example of the government’s outsized control over Iranians’ daily lives, which are hampered by crippling economic sanctions.
How did Iran become so stringent?
A paramilitary was charged in 1979 with safeguarding the Islamic Revolution and its values. Now it’s a massive complex and deeply entrenched: the Revolution is, in a sense, too big to fail.
Whose iron grip?
The revolution was supposed to be democratic. But a militaristic man made sure he had all the power.
What comes next?
Manipulation of the election process has placed hardliners in firm control. They’re ramping up spending on regime security, and investing little in the needs of their people.
Is there hope for change?
A former prime minister is one of the leading voices for greater personal freedoms, fighting deep-rooted corruption, and improving human rights. He’s been under house arrest since 2011 (and so has his ally, the reformist cleric Mehdi Karroubi).
